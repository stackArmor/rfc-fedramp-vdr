\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Determining whether a vulnerability is internet-reachable is one of the most consequential steps in scoping mitigation and remediation timeframes under the FedRAMP Vulnerability Detection and Response (VDR) and Vulnerability Evaluation and Reporting (VER) rules, launched on 2026-06-24 as part of the FedRAMP Consolidated Rules for 2026 and applicable to both 20x and Rev5 offerings. The Internet-Reachable Vulnerability (IRV) determination (\code{FRD-IRV}, \code{VER-EVA-EIR}) separates vulnerabilities on the tightest \code{VDR-TFR-PVR} clocks from Not Internet-reachable Vulnerabilities (NIRV). Critically, FedRAMP defines reachability as a property of \emph{vulnerabilities}, not of network topology: a database on a private subnet with no internet route still hosts internet-reachable SQL-injection vulnerabilities if it processes payloads that originated on the public internet, so methods that compute only the internet-\emph{accessibility} of resources produce exactly the false negatives the definition exists to prevent. This paper proposes a deterministic, three-phase \textbf{Exclusion-Gated Reachability Method}: Phase~A computes direct exposure of assets through four exclusion gates (routing, firewall source-population attribution, remote-access boundary authentication, and container routing) plus a process-level refinement; Phase~B propagates \emph{transitive payload exposure} across the observed data-flow graph, pruning assets that no internet-derived data can reach; and Phase~C applies three per-vulnerability filters---execution evidence, trigger-surface matching, and trigger-mechanism class---to the vulnerabilities on payload-exposed assets. Every filter defaults to ``reachable'' absent deterministic evidence, and every exclusion maps to a machine-readable Vulnerability Exploitability eXchange (VEX) justification. Finally, we revisit the Web Application Firewall (WAF) question: FedRAMP explicitly permits treating perimeter-intercepted vulnerabilities as no longer internet-reachable, but we recommend the stricter posture of retaining IRV status internally and attesting mitigation via signed VEX---noting the \code{VDR-TFR-KEV} carve-out that Known Exploited Vulnerabilities must still be remediated on CISA BOD 26-04 due dates even when fully mitigated.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
On 2026-06-24, FedRAMP launched the Consolidated Rules for 2026, including the Vulnerability Detection and Response (VDR) and Vulnerability Evaluation and Reporting (VER) rule sets. These rules apply to both FedRAMP 20x and Rev5 offerings, and they replace scanner-centric continuous-monitoring guidance with an evaluation-driven model: providers \rfckw{must} evaluate every detected vulnerability to determine whether it is an Internet-Reachable Vulnerability (IRV) per \code{VER-EVA-EIR}, whether it is a Likely Exploitable Vulnerability (LEV) per \code{VER-EVA-ELX}, and what Potential Agency Impact N-rating (PAIN) exploitation could carry per \code{VER-EVA-EPA}. Those three determinations jointly select a column and row in the \code{VDR-TFR-PVR} mitigation-and-remediation timeframe matrix, and each must be reported per vulnerability under \code{VER-RPT-VDT}.

A mistake in the reachability determination is costly in either direction. Over-classifying internal vulnerabilities as IRV floods the security team with artificial emergencies and compresses timeframes that the rules did not intend to compress. Under-classifying genuinely reachable vulnerabilities as NIRV---FedRAMP's term for a Not Internet-reachable Vulnerability (\code{FRD-IRV})---leaves exploitable paths open past their intended clocks, and \code{VER-EVA-ELX} warns that evaluations made ``recklessly or deliberately'' wrong will damage a provider's FedRAMP Certification.

The challenge is that modern cloud architectures are dynamic and multi-layered. Blanket assumptions---declaring every asset in a public subnet or every container in a GKE/EKS cluster internet-reachable---are too coarse, while manual ad-hoc declarations are unverifiable and will not survive rigorous 3PAO or agency review. This paper proposes an \textbf{Exclusion-Gated Reachability Method}: every vulnerability is assumed internet-reachable \emph{by default} and is excluded only when deterministic, auditable evidence rules it out. The method is organized in three phases, described in Section~\ref{sec:method}.

A prior draft of this method---like most tooling in the market today---computed the internet-\emph{accessibility} of resources and stopped there. Section~\ref{sec:vuln-not-resource} explains why that is not the question FedRAMP asks, and why answering the wrong question produces false negatives on precisely the vulnerability classes (SQL injection, Log4Shell) that FedRAMP's own rule notes use as motivating examples.

\section{Reachability Attaches to Vulnerabilities, Not Resources}
\label{sec:vuln-not-resource}
FedRAMP defines an Internet-Reachable Vulnerability (\code{FRD-IRV}) as:
\begin{quote}
``A vulnerability in a machine-based information resource that might be exploited or otherwise triggered by a payload originating from a source on the public internet.''
\end{quote}
The definition's notes remove any ambiguity about topology: it explicitly ``includes machine-based information resources that have no direct route to/from the internet but receive payloads or otherwise take action triggered by internet activity,'' while also limiting scope in the other direction---``internet-reachability applies only to the specific vulnerable machine-based information resources processing the payload.''

The evaluation rule \code{VER-EVA-EIR} states the design intent directly: ``FedRAMP focuses on internet-reachable (rather than internet-accessible) to ensure that any service that might receive a payload from the internet is prioritized if that service has a vulnerability that can be triggered by processing the data in the payload.'' Its notes supply two canonical examples. The first is SQL injection, ``where an application stack behind a load balancer and firewall with no ability to route traffic to or from the internet can receive a payload indirectly from the internet that triggers the manipulation or compromise of data in a database that can only be accessed by an authorized connection from the application server on a private network.'' The second is Log4Shell, ``where exploitation was possible via vulnerable internet-reachable resources deep in the application stack that were often not internet-accessible themselves.''

These examples are fatal to any method that equates reachability with accessibility. A routing-and-firewall analysis will correctly conclude that the private-subnet database is not internet-accessible---and will therefore mark its vulnerabilities NIRV, which is exactly the false negative \code{FRD-IRV} exists to prevent. Consistent with \code{VER-EVA-EFA}, which lists \emph{Reachability} (``How might a threat actor reach the vulnerability and how likely is that?'') among the evaluation factors alongside exploitability, prevalence, and privilege, a sound method must answer a question about payload flow, not packet routing:

\begin{quote}
\emph{Can data that originated on the public internet arrive---directly or through any chain of intermediaries---at the vulnerable code, in a form that could trigger the vulnerability?}
\end{quote}

Accessibility analysis remains necessary---it identifies the entry points where internet data comes ashore---but it is only the first phase of the determination, not the determination itself.

\begin{plain}
The question is not ``can the internet connect to this server?'' but ``can data that started on the internet ever reach this flaw?'' A database with no internet route still hosts internet-reachable injection flaws, because the attacker's input walks in through the public application and is handed to the database by your own tier-to-tier plumbing. Tools that stop at server exposure answer the wrong question --- and go wrong on exactly the cases FedRAMP names (SQL injection, Log4Shell).
\end{plain}

\section{Scope Realism: What the IRV Determination Actually Controls}
\label{sec:scope}
Before presenting the method, it is worth being precise about the stakes, because the IRV determination alone does not set the tight clock. The \code{VDR-TFR-PVR} timeframe matrix is indexed by \emph{three} evaluations jointly---PAIN rating (rows) and the combination of likely exploitability and internet reachability (columns LEV\,+\,IRV, LEV\,+\,NIRV, and NLEV). Table~\ref{tab:pain} reproduces the Class~C matrix, likely the most common certification class.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{PAIN Rating} & \textbf{LEV + IRV} & \textbf{LEV + NIRV} & \textbf{NLEV} \\
\midrule
PAIN-5 & 2 days & 4 days & 16 days \\
PAIN-4 & 4 days & 8 days & 64 days \\
PAIN-3 & 16 days & 32 days & 128 days \\
PAIN-2 & 48 days & 128 days & 192 days \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{\code{VDR-TFR-PVR} mitigation/remediation timeframes for Class C certifications. Classes A and B share a more generous schedule on the same structure; Class D is tighter.}
\label{tab:pain}
\end{table}

Three observations keep the reachability problem in proportion:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
\item \textbf{LEV gates the tight columns.} The IRV and NIRV columns only differ for vulnerabilities that are also likely exploitable. \code{VER-EVA-ELX} is blunt that this is a minority: ``the simple reality is that most traditional vulnerabilities discovered by scanners or during assessment are not likely to be exploitable.'' The reachability determination matters most for the LEV subset---though note that reachability feeds back into the LEV determination itself, since \code{FRD-LEV} requires the vulnerability to be ``reachable by a likely threat actor,'' and its floor note makes any vulnerability ``that an automated unauthenticated system can exploit over the internet'' automatically LEV.
\item \textbf{The timeframes are \rfckw{should}-level and are satisfied by mitigation.} \code{VDR-TFR-PVR} asks providers to ``partially mitigate, fully mitigate, or remediate'' \emph{to a lower Potential Agency Impact N-rating} within the timeframe. A partial mitigation that demonstrably reduces the PAIN rating satisfies that timeframe---but it does not end the process: the finding continues on the new, longer rating's clock, with the next reduction reported under \code{VER-RPT-VDT}. The descent is bounded rather than endless: it terminates in remediation, full mitigation, PAIN-1 (which carries no timeframe), or formal acceptance---\code{VER-TFR-MAV} requires any vulnerability that will not be fully mitigated or remediated within 192 days of evaluation to be categorized as an accepted vulnerability. Full remediation is not required within any single timeframe (with the KEV exception discussed in Section~\ref{sec:waf}).
\item \textbf{The goal is determinism, not deadline-shrinking.} The purpose of the method in this paper is not to minimize the IRV population by aggressive interpretation. It is to make the determination deterministic, evidence-driven, and auditable, so that the LEV\,+\,IRV set a provider reports under \code{VER-RPT-VDT} is one it can defend line by line to a 3PAO, an agency, or FedRAMP itself.
\end{itemize}

\begin{plain}
Reachability is one of three dials, and rarely decisive alone: the tightest clocks bind only flaws that are \emph{also} likely exploitable --- a minority --- and each clock is satisfied by demonstrably knocking the risk down a rung, not only by a full fix. So the goal here is not to argue findings off the urgent list; it is to make every reachability call one you can prove to an auditor.
\end{plain}

\section{Method Overview: Three Phases}
\label{sec:method}
The method evaluates the pair $(v, a)$---vulnerability $v$ on asset $a$---through three phases, illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:phases}.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\begin{minipage}{0.92\textwidth}
\centering
\textbf{Default state: every vulnerability on every asset is assumed internet-reachable (IRV).}
\medskip

\fbox{\begin{minipage}{0.95\textwidth}
\textbf{Phase A --- Direct Exposure $E(a)$} \emph{(asset-level; identifies entry points)}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\bfseries Gate \arabic*:, leftmargin=4.5em, itemsep=2pt, nosep]
\item \textnormal{IP and routing: no public IP / NAT / LB mapping and no internet-gateway route.}
\item \textnormal{Firewall source population: all permitted sources enumerable and attributable to identified organizations.}
\item \textnormal{Remote-access boundary authentication: the edge rejects unauthenticated traffic \emph{and} fronts a restricted, organization-controlled workforce population (not the service's customers).}
\item \textnormal{Kubernetes/container routing: no public Gateway route, Ingress, NodePort, \code{hostNetwork}/\code{hostPort}, or LoadBalancer exposure.}
\end{enumerate}
\textnormal{Plus a process-level refinement (Gate 5): software that binds no exposed listening socket is not \emph{directly} exposed. Phase A output feeds Phase B; \textbf{it excludes nothing from IRV scope by itself.}}
\end{minipage}}

\medskip $\downarrow$ \medskip

\fbox{\begin{minipage}{0.95\textwidth}
\textbf{Phase B --- Transitive Payload Exposure $T(a)$} \emph{(asset-level; data-flow graph)}\\
\textnormal{Propagate taint from Phase A entry points across observed data flows (NetworkPolicies + flow telemetry). Assets with \emph{no} flow path from any entry point are pruned: all their vulnerabilities are NIRV. Tainted assets proceed to Phase C.}
\end{minipage}}

\medskip $\downarrow$ \medskip

\fbox{\begin{minipage}{0.95\textwidth}
\textbf{Phase C --- Per-Vulnerability Filters} \emph{(vulnerability-level, on tainted assets)}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\bfseries Filter \arabic*:, leftmargin=4.5em, itemsep=2pt, nosep]
\item \textnormal{Execution evidence: vulnerable code not present in, or never loaded into, the execute path.}
\item \textnormal{Trigger-surface matching: required protocol/format never delivered on any inbound tainted edge.}
\item \textnormal{Trigger-mechanism class: peer-bound vulnerability classes on assets that are not directly exposed.}
\end{enumerate}
\end{minipage}}

\medskip

\textbf{Final classification: vulnerabilities surviving all three phases are IRV.}
\end{minipage}
\caption{The three-phase exclusion-gated method. Phase A computes direct exposure, Phase B prunes assets unreachable by internet-derived data, and Phase C filters individual vulnerabilities on payload-exposed assets.}
\label{fig:phases}
\end{figure}

The phases compose into a single decision rule. Let $E(a)$ denote that asset $a$ is directly exposed (survives Phase A), $T(a)$ that $a$ is transitively payload-exposed (Phase B), and let $\mathrm{exec}(v,a)$, $\mathrm{surface}(v,a)$, and $\mathrm{mech}(v,a)$ denote that Phase C filters 1--3 respectively \emph{apply} (i.e., fail to exclude). Then:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{IRV}(v,a) \;=\; \bigl[\,E(a) \;\lor\; T(a)\,\bigr] \;\land\; \mathrm{exec}(v,a) \;\land\; \mathrm{surface}(v,a) \;\land\; \mathrm{mech}(v,a)
\label{eq:decision}
\end{equation}
Every term defaults to \emph{true} absent deterministic evidence to the contrary. This gives the method its central soundness property: incomplete telemetry, unknown trigger surfaces, or unclassifiable vulnerabilities can only \emph{inflate} the IRV set, never silently shrink it.

\begin{plain}
Every flaw starts out presumed internet-reachable. It leaves that list only by passing hard-evidence checks: no internet-derived data ever reaches its host (Phases A--B), or the data that does arrive can never trigger this particular flaw (Phase C). Missing telemetry or an unrecognized bug class defaults to ``still reachable'' --- so evidence gaps make the urgent list longer, never quietly shorter.
\end{plain}

\section{Phase A: Direct Exposure Gates}
\label{sec:phasea}
Phase A answers the classical accessibility question: which assets can a source on the public internet deliver packets to directly? Its output is the set of \emph{entry points}---the places where internet data comes ashore. To be explicit about the restructure motivated in Section~\ref{sec:vuln-not-resource}: \textbf{Phase A identifies entry points for Phase B's taint propagation; failing a Phase A gate does not, by itself, exclude anything from IRV scope.} The private-subnet database in FedRAMP's SQL-injection example fails Gate 1 and is still IRV for its injection vulnerabilities, because Phase B will find the payload path through the application tier.

\subsection{Gate 1: IP Address and Routing}
An asset is directly exposed at the network layer only if two preconditions hold:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
\item The asset holds a routable public IP address, or belongs to a NAT or load-balancer pool that maps public traffic to it.
\item The subnet's routing table has an active route to an internet gateway (\code{0.0.0.0/0} via an IGW or equivalent).
\end{enumerate}
Assets failing either condition---private and isolated subnets, egress-only NAT configurations---are not entry points. They remain fully eligible for transitive payload exposure in Phase B.

\subsection{Gate 2: Firewall Source Population and Attribution}
Even with a public IP and an internet route, an asset whose inbound rules restrict sources to specific known networks is not exposed to the general internet. An earlier draft of this method used a size heuristic: exclusion whenever every permitted source prefix was \code{/20} or smaller. That heuristic is unsound in both directions. It is gameable---one hundred individual \code{/24} rules each pass the per-rule test while their union covers more address space than a \code{/16}---and it produces false positives, since a single legitimately allowlisted partner \code{/16} fails the test despite representing exactly one attributable organization.

The corrected gate evaluates, per listening port, the \emph{union} of all permitted source address space across every firewall rule, security group, and WAF-layer IP restriction that governs it, and applies an ownership criterion:
\begin{quote}
\textbf{Exclusion criterion.} The asset/port is excluded from direct exposure if and only if the union of permitted sources is enumerable and every constituent range is attributable to an identified organization under an agreement with the provider---corporate networks, named partners, or specific customer egress ranges on record.
\end{quote}
Aggregate size is retained only as a red flag: a union exceeding a \code{/20}-equivalent (4{,}096 addresses) \rfckw{should} trigger review of the attribution records, because allowlists of that size frequently accrete unattributed ranges. But size is a tie-breaker for scrutiny, not the definition: a fully attributed partner \code{/16} passes; a hundred anonymous \code{/24}s do not.

\begin{plain}
An allowlist earns an exclusion by being \emph{accounted for}, not by being small. If every permitted address range can be traced to a named organization you have a relationship with, the door is closed to the general internet; if parts of the allowlist are anonymous, it is not --- no matter how narrow the individual ranges look.
\end{plain}

\subsection{Gate 3: Remote-Access Boundaries (Edge Authentication)}
\label{sec:gate3}
Many architectures place backends behind a boundary that authenticates traffic at the edge: identity-aware proxies (Google Identity-Aware Proxy, AWS Verified Access, ALB OIDC/Cognito authentication) or client VPNs. Both technology families reject unauthenticated traffic before the backend processes it, and both are conventionally treated very differently---backends behind a VPN are habitually called ``internal,'' while backends behind an IAP are often scored as exposed. This gate unifies them under a single functional test:
\begin{quote}
\emph{Edge authentication qualifies for exclusion from direct exposure when it functions as \textbf{remote-access control} for a restricted workforce population---the clientless-VPN role, fronting admin consoles, operations tooling, and internal dashboards. It does not qualify when it functions as \textbf{customer authentication} for the service itself.}
\end{quote}

Rejecting unauthenticated payloads before the vulnerable resource processes them genuinely satisfies the interception principle in \code{VER-EVA-EIR}: ``the simplest way to prevent exploitation of internet-reachable vulnerabilities is to intercept, inspect, filter, sanitize, reject, or otherwise deflect triggering payloads before they are processed by the vulnerable resource; once this prevention is in place the vulnerability should no longer be considered an internet-reachable vulnerability.'' For \emph{unauthenticated} payloads, an enforcing IAP or VPN is precisely such a prevention. The residual question is therefore: \emph{who can still deliver payloads after authenticating?} The remote-access role answers ``a restricted, enumerable workforce''; the customer-authentication role answers ``the service's internet user base.'' In verifiable form:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Exclusion criterion.} A vulnerability $v$ on backend asset $a$ may be excluded from direct exposure via edge authentication if and only if all of the following hold:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*), leftmargin=2.5em, nosep]
\item the boundary rejects unauthenticated traffic before $v$'s vulnerable code processes any attacker-controlled data;
\item the authorized principal set is restricted, enumerable, and organization-controlled (enrolled devices, provisioned or device-bound credentials, phishing-resistant MFA), not open enrollment or the service's general internet user base; and
\item the backend verifiably validates the boundary's cryptographic identity assertion (e.g., validating the IAP-signed JWT on every request), so that header spoofing or misrouting around the boundary does not silently void the exclusion.
\end{enumerate}
\end{quote}

Condition (b) is the remote-access test in checkable form, and it is where the conventional intuitions succeed and fail. ``Behind the VPN, therefore internal'' silently relies on it: the assumption is usually true because VPNs typically front an enrolled workforce with provisioned credentials. An IAP policy restricted to specific principals with device trust is the zero-trust equivalent of that configuration and earns the same exclusion. Conversely, when (a) holds but (b) fails---edge authentication serving as the front door of the \emph{customer-facing application} for its general user base---internet-originated payloads still flow through authentication and are processed by the backend. SQL injection behind IAP is still an IRV; SQL injection behind a customer VPN with ten thousand internet-based users is equally still an IRV. In that configuration, edge authentication is properly modeled as an \emph{exploitability} input rather than a reachability exclusion: it defeats the \code{FRD-LEV} floor (``any vulnerability that an automated unauthenticated system can exploit over the internet is a likely exploitable vulnerability''), which may support an NLEV determination under \code{VER-EVA-ELX}, but it does not make the payload path disappear.

\begin{plain}
A login wall in front of your \emph{staff} tools works like a VPN: only enrolled employees can deliver data past it, so what sits behind it is not internet-reachable. The same login wall as the front door of your \emph{customer} product excludes nothing --- anyone on the internet can hold an account and send hostile input straight through it. What matters is who can get past the door, not the door itself. (Either way, the door --- the proxy or VPN concentrator itself --- stays on the internet-reachable list.)
\end{plain}

Two practical notes calibrate how often the distinction bites:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
\item \textbf{The disqualifying configuration is rare in FedRAMP practice.} Identity-aware proxies are overwhelmingly deployed in the qualifying remote-access role; edge authentication deployed as the customer-facing front door of the application itself is uncommon. The test therefore rarely burdens a real deployment. Its function is that of an anti-loophole guardrail: without condition (b), the rule collapses into ``any login page renders the stack NIRV,'' which would make post-authentication SQL injection unreachable on paper---indefensible under \code{FRD-IRV}, and the first loophole an assessor would probe.
\item \textbf{The population property is largely machine-checkable.} Whether the principal set is restricted and organization-controlled can often be derived from provider APIs---for example, GCP IAP IAM bindings with enumerable principals and no \code{allUsers}/\code{allAuthenticatedUsers} members---so automation can decide this gate where such a signal exists; boundaries without a reliable signal resolve through an explicit, recorded operator attestation. The full detection design appears in the companion tooling specification.
\end{itemize}

Two residuals apply in every configuration:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
\item \textbf{The boundary itself remains IRV.} The VPN concentrator, the load balancer plus IAP stack, and any TLS-termination and HTTP-parsing surface that runs before authentication are directly exposed by construction; their vulnerabilities cannot be excluded by the mechanism they implement.
\item \textbf{The L3/L7 asymmetry matters for scoping the residual.} A network-layer VPN blocks unauthenticated sources before a TCP connection ever completes to the backend, so the pre-authentication attack surface is the concentrator alone. An IAP enforces at L7: the cloud load balancer has already terminated TLS and parsed HTTP before the access decision, so the pre-authentication surface includes the provider's LB/proxy parsing stack. The exclusion for the backend can be identical; the inventory of what stays IRV differs.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Gate 4: Kubernetes and Container Routing}
For containerized workloads, direct exposure must be evaluated at the workload level, since containers are decoupled from node-level network exposure. A pod is a direct-exposure entry point if and only if it is the active target of at least one of:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*), leftmargin=2.5em, nosep]
\item a Gateway API route (\code{HTTPRoute}, \code{TCPRoute}, etc.) bound to a public-facing Gateway;
\item an \code{Ingress} bound to a public load balancer;
\item a \code{Service} of type \code{LoadBalancer} with a public address;
\item a \code{NodePort} service on nodes reachable from the internet; or
\item \code{hostNetwork:\ true} or a \code{hostPort} mapping on a node with public exposure---this includes DaemonSets, which frequently run with host networking on every node and are easy to overlook in route-centric inventories.
\end{enumerate}
Pods exposed only through internal load balancers or cluster-internal Services are not entry points (but remain Phase B candidates). Two verifiability caveats:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
\item \textbf{NodePort is not decidable from inside the cluster.} A NodePort binds on every node, but whether internet sources can reach it depends on node public IPs and cloud firewall rules---data outside the Kubernetes API. The gate \rfckw{must} join cluster state with the node-level Gate 1/Gate 2 evaluation, or fall back to an explicit, recorded operator assertion; absent either, the NodePort is treated as exposed.
\item \textbf{Init containers.} Within an exposed pod, \code{initContainers} run to completion before primary containers start and hold no listening sockets during steady state; they are not directly exposed \emph{unless} declared with \code{restartPolicy:\ Always}, in which case they are long-running sidecars and are treated as primary containers.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Gate 5: Process-to-Port Refinement (and Its Limit)}
On a directly exposed host, not every installed package is directly exposed. Software \rfckw{may} be excluded from \emph{direct} exposure when it can be deterministically mapped to running processes that bind no internet-exposed listening socket---for example, \code{sshd} vulnerabilities on a public web server whose port 22 is restricted to attributed sources under Gate 2. Such mapping (package manifest joined with socket-ownership telemetry) \rfckw{must} be automation-driven and reproducible per asset.

The limit of this gate is the central lesson of Section~\ref{sec:vuln-not-resource}, so we state the counterexample explicitly: a queue worker on a private VM with \emph{no listening socket at all} that parses internet-uploaded files---a thumbnail generator, a log ingester, exactly the Log4Shell shape---is IRV for its parser vulnerabilities. ``Binds no exposed port'' excludes a vulnerability only in combination with the Phase B condition ``consumes no internet-tainted data stream.'' Gate 5 is a refinement of direct exposure, never a standalone NIRV determination.

\section{Phase B: Transitive Payload Exposure}
\label{sec:phaseb}
Phase B operationalizes the \code{FRD-IRV} note that reachability ``includes machine-based information resources that have no direct route to/from the internet but receive payloads or otherwise take action triggered by internet activity.'' The object of analysis is the \emph{data-flow graph}, not the routing graph: nodes are assets (or workloads), and a directed edge $a \rightarrow b$ exists when data observed to leave $a$ arrives at and is processed by $b$---requests, messages, files, replication streams. An asset is \textbf{payload-exposed} (tainted), written $T(a)$, if there is any directed path to it from any Phase A entry point.

Perfect data-flow analysis is intractable; the method instead uses a deterministic over-approximation built from two evidence classes:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
\item \textbf{Kubernetes estates:} the union of (i) flows admitted by NetworkPolicies (the declared graph) and (ii) flows actually observed by CNI or mesh telemetry such as Cilium Hubble or service-mesh (e.g., Istio) flow logs (the observed graph). Where NetworkPolicies are absent or default-allow, the observed graph governs, and the permissive posture itself is a finding.
\item \textbf{Non-Kubernetes estates:} VPC flow logs joined with host-level socket and peer telemetry (which process held the socket, which peers it exchanged data with), yielding per-process flow edges rather than mere IP adjacency.
\end{itemize}
Taint then propagates by fixed-point iteration: seed the entry-point set from Phase A, add any asset with an inbound edge from a tainted asset, repeat until stable. Because the flow evidence is captured continuously, the graph---and therefore the IRV determination---re-derives automatically as the architecture changes, consistent with the automation posture of \code{VDR-CSO-ADT}.

Phase B is the method's one wholesale pruning step, and it is deliberately conservative in structure:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
\item \textbf{Assets with no path are NIRV wholesale.} If no flow path exists from any entry point to asset $a$ over the full observation window---an isolated batch cluster, an air-gapped management enclave, a database reachable only from a non-tainted internal tool---then no payload originating on the internet can arrive at $a$, and \emph{every} vulnerability on $a$ is NIRV. The retained flow logs and policy snapshots are the audit evidence for the exclusion.
\item \textbf{Assets with a path are not thereby condemned.} Taint means internet-derived data reaches the asset in some form; it does not mean every vulnerability on the asset can be triggered by it. Tainted assets proceed to the per-vulnerability filters of Phase C.
\item \textbf{Direction and decay matter.} Edges are directional (an asset that only \emph{pulls} from a tainted source still receives its data and is tainted; an asset that only \emph{pushes} to one is not tainted by that edge), and observed-flow evidence \rfckw{must} carry a bounded observation window so that stale, since-removed flows age out of the graph.
\end{itemize}

\begin{plain}
Phase B follows the data, not the network diagram. Wherever internet-originated data flows --- requests, queued messages, uploaded files, replication --- every system it touches stays in scope. A system your flow records show internet data never reaches drops out entirely, flaws and all, with those records as the audit evidence. This is the method's only wholesale exclusion, and it rests on observed absence of a data path, not on assumptions about the architecture.
\end{plain}

\section{Phase C: Per-Vulnerability Filters on Payload-Exposed Assets}
\label{sec:phasec}
Phase C restores precision on tainted assets, honoring the \code{FRD-IRV} note that ``internet-reachability applies only to the specific vulnerable machine-based information resources processing the payload.'' Each filter is a deterministic exclusion with a defined evidence source and a corresponding VEX justification (per the companion paper, \emph{A CycloneDX VEX Profile for FedRAMP Rev5 VDR/VER}); each defaults to ``applies'' (vulnerability remains IRV) when evidence is absent or ambiguous.

\subsection{Filter 1: Execution Evidence}
The strongest and most general filter asks whether the vulnerable code can execute at all. Two evidence tiers:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
\item \textbf{Package never executed:} the vulnerable package is installed (visible to scanners) but no process from it has run during the observation window---build-time residue, disabled agents, unused OS components. VEX justification: \code{code\_not\_present}.
\item \textbf{Symbol never in the execute path:} the library loads, but runtime telemetry (e.g., eBPF-based loaded-library and symbol-resolution tracing) shows the vulnerable function is never in the execute path. VEX justification: \code{code\_not\_reachable}.
\end{itemize}
(The corresponding CISA status-justification labels, under the companion profile's optional crosswalk, are \code{vulnerable\_code\_not\_present} and \code{vulnerable\_code\_not\_in\_execute\_path} respectively.)
This filter is class-agnostic---it applies equally to injection flaws, parser bugs, and protocol flaws---which makes it the highest-yield filter to automate. It generalizes Gate 5: where Gate 5 excludes only direct exposure of non-listening processes, execution evidence excludes triggering outright, regardless of how tainted data arrives.

\subsection{Filter 2: Trigger-Surface Matching}
A vulnerability requires a specific trigger surface: a protocol, file format, or API endpoint whose processing exercises the flaw. Phase B's edges carry more than adjacency---the flow telemetry identifies what is actually delivered on each edge (protocol, port, often application protocol from mesh or eBPF-level classification). Filter 2 intersects the two:
\begin{quote}
\textbf{Exclusion criterion.} Vulnerability $v$ on tainted asset $a$ is excluded if the trigger surface required by $v$ has an empty intersection with the set of data formats and protocols actually delivered on $a$'s inbound tainted edges.
\end{quote}
Example: an SMB-stack CVE on a host whose only inbound tainted edge carries PostgreSQL wire protocol from the application tier is excludable---no internet-derived payload arrives in a form the SMB code will ever parse. The mapping from CVE to required surface comes from enrichment data (affected component, CPE, vendor advisory); where the required surface cannot be determined, \textbf{the filter defaults to a wildcard surface and the vulnerability stays IRV}. This default is what gives the filter its minimal-false-negative property: incomplete knowledge widens the IRV set rather than narrowing it.

\subsection{Filter 3: Trigger-Mechanism Class}
The final filter partitions vulnerability classes by \emph{how the trigger arrives}, keyed on CWE (available from NVD enrichment of CVE records):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
\item \textbf{Payload-carried} classes are triggered by the \emph{content} of data, which survives any number of forwarding hops: injection (CWE-89 SQL, CWE-78 OS command, CWE-917 expression language---the Log4Shell mechanism), deserialization of untrusted data (CWE-502), memory corruption in format parsers (CWE-119/CWE-787 where the component parses delivered data), XXE (CWE-611), SSRF (CWE-918), and path traversal (CWE-22). These are precisely FedRAMP's SQL-injection and Log4Shell concern: \textbf{payload-carried vulnerabilities remain IRV wherever tainted data flows and can never be excluded by class.} They may still be excluded by Filters 1 or 2.
\item \textbf{Peer-bound} classes require the attacker to be the \emph{direct network peer} of the vulnerable listener: authentication bypass at the socket (CWE-287/CWE-306 on the listening service itself), TLS and protocol handshake or state-machine flaws, and connection-flood denial of service. A forwarded application payload can never exercise a handshake or open a raw connection to an interior listener. \textbf{Peer-bound vulnerabilities are deterministically excludable on assets that are not directly exposed} ($\lnot E(a)$): the only peers that can reach the listener are the interior systems Phase B identified, none of which is an internet source.
\end{itemize}
Two cautions prevent the naive misuse of this filter. First, stored XSS is payload-carried: the script content originates from the internet and is faithfully forwarded through the stack even though it ultimately detonates in a browser; ``XSS is client-side, therefore excluded'' is unsound. Second, not all DoS is peer-bound: ReDoS and decompression bombs (zip bombs) are triggered by payload content and follow the data wherever it flows. A blanket class exclusion of ``XSS and DoS'' would therefore be exactly the kind of reckless evaluation \code{VER-EVA-ELX} warns against. The sound unit of exclusion is the triple \emph{class $\times$ topological position $\times$ taint}, as expressed in Equation~\ref{eq:decision}; vulnerabilities whose CWE is missing or maps to neither partition default to payload-carried and stay IRV.

\begin{plain}
Two kinds of bugs. Some are set off by the \emph{content} of the data --- a poisoned string, a booby-trapped file --- and those travel with the data through any number of internal hops, so they stay in scope wherever tainted data goes. Others require the attacker to be the machine at the \emph{other end of the connection} --- handshake, login-at-the-socket, and connection-flood flaws --- and those can be crossed off on interior systems, whose only peers are your own machines. Sorting a bug into the wrong pile (``XSS is client-side,'' ``DoS is peer-bound'') is exactly the reckless shortcut the rules warn against, so anything unclassifiable is treated as content-triggered.
\end{plain}

\section{Perimeter Mitigation: Permitted Downgrade, Recommended Attestation}
\label{sec:waf}
The final design question is how to represent vulnerabilities shielded by a Web Application Firewall or comparable payload-inspecting perimeter (virtual patches, OWASP core rule set deployments). Two paradigms compete:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, nosep]
\item \textbf{The Accessibility Paradigm:} treat the WAF rule as removing internet reachability---reclassify the vulnerability NIRV.
\item \textbf{The Attestation Paradigm:} keep the vulnerability classified IRV, and publish a signed Vulnerability Exploitability eXchange (VEX) attestation that it is mitigated at the perimeter.
\end{enumerate}

An earlier draft of this paper argued that the Attestation Paradigm was the only audit-compliant option and that reclassifying WAF-shielded vulnerabilities as NIRV would draw non-compliance findings. The launched rules say otherwise, and we correct the record: the \code{VER-EVA-EIR} interception note quoted in Section~\ref{sec:gate3} states that once payload interception ``is in place the vulnerability should no longer be considered an internet-reachable vulnerability.'' A WAF rule that verifiably intercepts the triggering payload class before the vulnerable resource processes it \emph{is} such a prevention. \textbf{FedRAMP explicitly permits the reachability downgrade.} The rules also soften the underlying tension through their mitigation vocabulary: FedRAMP formally distinguishes mitigation from remediation (\code{VDR-CSO-RES}: ``a fully mitigated vulnerability will still exist (with negligible risk) until it has been remediated''; see \code{FRD-PMV}, \code{FRD-FMV}), and the \code{VDR-TFR-PVR} timeframes are satisfied by mitigation to a lower PAIN rating. A WAF virtual patch is a legitimate partial or full mitigation on its own terms, with or without any reclassification.

We nonetheless recommend the Attestation Paradigm as the stricter and operationally safer posture, for reasons that are practical rather than regulatory:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
\item \textbf{WAF exclusions are fragile in a way topology exclusions are not.} A Phase B exclusion rests on the absence of a data path; a WAF exclusion rests on a signature matching every variant of an exploit. Rules are exploit-specific, bypasses are published routinely, and every rule change, CDN migration, or emergency ``detection-only'' toggle silently invalidates the downgrade. A provider that reclassifies NIRV on WAF evidence takes on a standing obligation to re-validate that classification on every perimeter change---an obligation that is easy to state and hard to operate.
\item \textbf{Misconfiguration re-exposes instantly.} If the WAF is bypassed or misrouted, a topology-based NIRV would still be true; a WAF-based NIRV becomes false the same moment, with no signal in the vulnerability record.
\item \textbf{Attestation is stronger audit evidence and preserves risk visibility.} Keeping the vulnerability IRV internally with a signed VEX record---\code{state: not\_affected} with \code{justification: protected\_at\_perimeter}, per the companion CycloneDX profile---gives the 3PAO a machine-readable, signed assertion naming the exact protecting control, while the provider's own dashboards continue to show the true residual risk. (A VEX document is a signed assertion by the security team, not a legal instrument; its value is cryptographic attributability and machine-readability, not liability transfer.)
\item \textbf{Distribution automates cleanly.} VEX attestations publish to container registries as OCI artifacts alongside images, or to object storage; vulnerability-management tooling fetches them at scan time and applies the mitigation status without mutating the reachability computation itself.
\end{itemize}

One carve-out is not optional under either paradigm. \code{VDR-TFR-KEV} provides that Known Exploited Vulnerabilities \rfckw{should} be \emph{remediated} according to the due dates in the CISA KEV catalog, ``even if the vulnerability has been fully mitigated,'' as required by CISA BOD 26-04. Log4Shell---the motivating example throughout this paper---is a KEV. A WAF virtual patch for a KEV, however effective, does not extend the BOD 26-04 remediation date, and neither does an NIRV reclassification. Providers \rfckw{must} track the KEV remediation clock independently of both the reachability determination and the mitigation status.

\begin{plain}
FedRAMP does allow you to stop counting a flaw as internet-reachable once a WAF rule verifiably blocks the payloads that trigger it. We recommend not taking the discount: WAF rules are bypassed, tuned, and toggled off far more easily than networks are re-plumbed. Keep the finding classified reachable and attach a signed, machine-readable note that it is mitigated at the perimeter --- your dashboards keep showing the true residual risk, and the auditor gets a stronger artifact. One thing no WAF and no reclassification can move: known-exploited (KEV) flaws must still be \emph{fixed} by CISA's due dates.
\end{plain}

\section{Summary and Conclusion}
The FedRAMP VDR/VER rules ask a sharper question than the vulnerability-management industry is accustomed to answering: not ``which resources are internet-accessible?'' but ``which \emph{vulnerabilities} can a payload originating on the public internet trigger?'' (\code{FRD-IRV}, \code{VER-EVA-EIR}). The Exclusion-Gated Reachability Method answers that question in three deterministic phases: Phase A's gates (routing, attributed firewall source populations, remote-access boundary authentication, container routing, and process-level refinement) identify where internet data enters; Phase B propagates payload taint across the observed data-flow graph and prunes---wholesale, with flow logs as evidence---the assets no internet-derived data can reach; and Phase C excludes individual vulnerabilities on tainted assets through execution evidence, trigger-surface matching, and the payload-carried/peer-bound mechanism partition. Every filter defaults toward IRV, so gaps in evidence surface as conservatism rather than as silent false negatives, and every exclusion carries machine-verifiable evidence and a VEX justification suitable for 3PAO and agency review.

The method's purpose is proportionality with defensibility. Most detected vulnerabilities are not likely exploitable (\code{VER-EVA-ELX}), the tight \code{VDR-TFR-PVR} clocks bind only the LEV\,+\,IRV intersection, and partial mitigation legitimately satisfies the timeframes---so a provider does not need to shrink its IRV set by fiat; it needs to be able to defend the set it reports. Where perimeter controls such as WAF virtual patches intercept triggering payloads, FedRAMP permits the reachability downgrade, but the stricter and more durable posture is to keep the vulnerability IRV and attest its mitigation with signed CycloneDX VEX---while honoring the \code{VDR-TFR-KEV} requirement to remediate Known Exploited Vulnerabilities on CISA BOD 26-04 due dates regardless of mitigation. Providers that implement the three phases as continuous automation obtain reachability determinations that re-derive as the architecture changes and that stand up, line by line, to the scrutiny the new rules invite.
